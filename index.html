About Me & My Journey

I am Luc Gerards, an Applied Data Science & AI student at Zuyd University of Applied Sciences, based in Maastricht. My journey into the world of tech started with a natural curiosity for problem-solving and a desire to uncover the hidden patterns behind complex systems.

Why this program?
I chose to study Applied Data Science & AI because I wanted to go beyond theoretical concepts and learn how to build tangible, intelligent systems that solve actual problems. I was fascinated by the idea of taking raw, chaotic data and engineering it into something that can predict outcomes, automate tasks, and drive real-world business decisions. The "Applied" aspect of my degree is incredibly important to me—I don't just want to understand how a machine learning algorithm works mathematically; I want to know how to containerize it, secure it, and deploy it for end-users.

True impact requires robust data engineering to build a reliable foundation, rigorous statistical modeling to uncover insights, and solid DevOps/AIOps principles to deploy those models reliably.

Predictive Modeling • 8-Week Team Project

Predicting Cyclists' Performance

Engineered an advanced Random Forest classification model for an investment firm (CAICLE) to predict professional cycling race outcomes for team recruitment.

PythonRandom ForestRFECV

Goal & Relevance

To accurately identify high-potential riders. This project demonstrates core data science capabilities: handling highly imbalanced datasets, strict temporal data splitting, and evaluating models based on business-aligned metrics.

Stakeholder Perspective

The client needed actionable, interpretable recruitment intelligence. This drove the decision to utilize a Random Forest model, which provided clear feature importance allowing the business to understand *why* a cyclist was flagged.

My Role & Responsibilities

Collaborated across the full CRISP-DM lifecycle. Conducted rigorous data preparation and temporal splitting to simulate a realistic prediction scenario, ensuring strictly no data leakage from future races.

Proudest Technical Decision

Feature Reduction & Calibration. I utilized Recursive Feature Elimination with Cross-Validation (RFECV) to objectively reduce the feature space from 40 to 15. Furthermore, applied CalibratedClassifierCV and threshold tuning to correct ensemble overconfidence and maximize the F1-score for our imbalanced dataset.
